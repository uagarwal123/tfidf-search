                        * What happens when the DV is categorical?
                        * Transition from absolute DV to prob so how likely it is that an event will occur 
                        * DV becomes P(y=1) -> prob is between 0 and 1 
                        * If we use normal regression with a binary DV, predictions can be <0 or >1 which is impossible 
                        * So instead use logistic equation to ensure that all values will fall between 0 and 1


                        * We model probabilities not outcomes directly 
                        * Since DV has to variance (only 0 or 1) we cannot use R squared 
                        * Instead use:
                        * Info on model fit: hit rate and pseudo R squared
                        * Testing model significance: chi square test 


                        * Model fit and testing: -2LL (log-likelihood)
                        * Logistic regression:predict prob of an event occurring for a given person based on obs whether or not even did occur for that person
                        * Observed Y: 0 or 1
                        * Predicted value: Between 0 and 1 
                        * Asses fit by summing prob associated with predicted and actual outcomes 
                        * Measures unexplained information after the model has been fitted 
                        * r values: better fit
                        * Larger values: bad fit as there is more unexplained observations 


                        * Model testing: chi square test:
                        * Equivilant to F-test in linear regression 
                        * F-test: does the model sig explain variance in Y 
                        * Chi-square test: does adding predictors significantly improve prediction compared to a model with no predictors
                        * Compare 2 models null (baseline) vs full to test if model is sig


                        * Model fit: pseudo R^2:
                        * Since it has no variance its not about explaining variance 
                        * Its the likelihood ratio of new/null model so basically how much better is the full model comapred to the null model 
                        * Pseudo R^2 values are much smaller than linear so 0.2-0.4 is a v strong model 
                        * The higher the better 
                        * Mianly useful to compare models 


                        * Model fit: hit rate/accuracy 
                        * For how many peiple is observed value Y = predicted value P(Y)

                        * Exaple for pass or fail you would use 0.5 but for cancer or not you would use 0,25 as cutoff
                        * Can also find the cutoff value to the % of observed events that have occurred 
                        * Accuracy = correct predictions / total

                        * exp(B): measures change in odds resulting from 1 unit change in predictor

                        * How to design product in such a way for consumers do buy the product 
                        * Conjoint analysis:
                        * Reason for that is preferences
                        * If you ask consumers about preferences, their stated preference is usually not the same as their revealed preference 
                        * If you ask about intention to buy, they are usually overconfident about buying prob 
                        * If u ask about self-reported importance weights, they identify everything is important
                        * In reality, they make tradeoffs between benefits and costs. So what they desire most and what they're willing to give up
                        * So conjoint analysis reveals true preferences and simulates realistic purchase decisions 
                        * How to set up conjoint analysis: 
                        * Give consumers tradeoffs instead of having everything 
                        * Consumers balance cost and benefit better than stating ideal points 
                        * Ideal points are generally not feasible 
                        * So use cost benefit trade offs as it helps us understand which tradeofs people are actually willing to make 
                        * Conjoint analysis: a quantitative survey-based marketing research technique designed to force consumers to make tradeoffs, thereby revealing true pref 
                        * It treats products or services as combinations of attributes a 
                        * estimates a mathematical model of consumers’ value systems allowing us to quantify  how much value consumers place on different attributes
                        * Also helps understand willingness to pay so what theyre willing to give uo to obtain a certain product
                        * Also distribution of willingness to pay which measures how consumers differ in their willingness to pay 
                        * It solves questions like what the customer wants? What is the expected market share?


                        * Basics of choice based conjoint (CBC)
                        * Questionnaire where respondents choose their preferred product from a set of choices with varying attributes
                        * Products are represented as bundles of attributes and each attribute has levels 
                        *                           * So you give the respondent n choice sets and in every one they pick their preferred option
                        * Because in every choice set theres a diff combination of attribute levels used, we can derive the effect of attribute level choice: attribute part-worths 


                           * Designing CBC study 
                           1. Choose study type: 
                           * Rating-based: rate every option 
                           * choice based: which one you would choose
                           * ranking based: rank most to least preferred
                           * Maxdiff: best and worst option 
                           2. Identify relevant attributes: which and how many 
                           * Should be relevant 
                           * Have varying levels 
                           * Not too many 
                           * Preferably not strongly correlated
                           3. Specify attribute levels: which and how many 
                           * Relevant to management 
                           * Unambiguous (high vs low is too imprecise)
                           * Separated enough but not too much (reasonable amount)
                           * Not too many
                           4. Design questionnaire: 
                           * Full factor design: include all possible combinations of attribute levels 
                           * Fractional: only includes a fraction of all possible combinations 
                           * Length of questionnaire: more choice tasks leads to more reliable estimates but too many decreases quality due to fatigue. Also, more levels means more parameters to be estimated so more obs needed. Rule of thumb: <=20 choice sets
                           * Number of alternatives: 2-4 alternatives per choice 
                           * Which stimuli to include: minimal level overlap, utility balance so equally attractive alt
                           * Fixed vs randomized designs: randomizing helps reduce effect of order 
                           * Adding no-choice option


                           * Data analysis:
                           *                              * Task: which choice set 
                           * Alt: which option within the choice set 
                              * Logistic regression:
                              * First create a dummy variable for each level of attribute minus the ref group
                              * It takes an option in the choice set and outputs the probability of how likely the person is to choose it 
                              * The B coeff is the log odds of choosing an attribute level compares to ref category, holding all other factors constant
                              * Using regular logistic regression is not ideal because in each choice set there are multiple alternatives. 
                              * Binary logit ignore that these three decisions are linked. It only takes into account that you are not choosing this option and not that if you choose this option you automatically dont choose the other options
                              * Consitional logit model is better as it calculates the prob of choosing a laptop relative to the other choices
                              * Analogy: Imagine three people running a race. Binary logit asks "will person A finish?" as if persons B and C don't exist. Conditional logit asks "given that A, B, and C are all running, what's the probability each one wins?"


                              *                                 * Ref category has Part-worth utility of 0
                              * part-worth utility: how much that attribute level increases or decreases the log odds of being chosen (preference)
                              * Higher B means its more preferred
                              * Negative N means that level is less preferred than the ref


                                 * To know how much each attribute matters overall you calculate attribute importance 
                                 * Range = max part-worth - min part-worth for that attribute 
                                 *   

                                    * Conditional logit models 
                                    * Provides a single set of utility estimates that are assumed to apply for everyone
                                    * assume all consumers values attributes the same way
                                    * Wtvr effect each attribute level has on choice probability it is fixed and constant across all respondents 




                                    * Mixed logit model
                                    * Can estimate part-worth utilities at the individual level by allowing part worths to vary randomly 
                                    * Recognizes that different individuals have different tastes 
                                    * Rather than giving you a single number per attribute, Mixed Logit gives you a whole distribution (a mean and a standard deviation) per attribute.
                                    *                                     *                                       * To check if there is heterogeneity, check the SD of the terms and the respective p-value -> if it it significant then there is heterogeneity 
                                    * In this example there is heteogeniety pref in brand and price but not processor


                                       * Using utility for managerial decisions:
                                       * Calculate total utility for each alternative by summing up part-worth utility. The one with the highest utility wins

                                                                  * If customers are bouncing across multiple touchpoints before they buy, which one gets the credit for the conversion 
                                                                  * Conversion: the point where the recipient of marketing messages does what you want 
                                                                  * Attribution: analytical method that reveals which marketing channels contributed ot that conversion
                                                                  * It matters because it directly affects how you allocate your marketing budget 
                                                                  * Research shopping: when customers use one channel for search and the other for shopping
                                                                  * webrooming: browse online then buy in store - useful for filters 
                                                                  * showrooming: browse in store then shop online 
                                                                  * Drivers of research shopping:
                                                                  * Attribute based decision making: one channel excels on attributes that determine search, while the other channel excels on attitude that drive purchase
                                                                  * Lack of channel lockin: lock in means higher attitude towards searching on channel A translate to higher attitudes toward purchasing on channel A. in reality for example you can search a lot on instagram but then actually buy in the store
                                                                  * Cross channel synergy: searching on channel A can actually enhance the experience of purchasing on channel B  
                                                                  *  This is why its hard to attribute credit correctly because if cross channel synergy exists you cant juts look at last touchpoint and attribute it to conversion. The earlier touchpoints were building the foundation for final purchase 
                                                                  * Solve the problem with carryover and spillover effects 
                                                                  * How much does each channel contribute 
                                                                  * Knowing this helps allocate marketing investments and target customers  
                                                                  * Example question attribute modeling answers
                                                                  * Which ads are driving conversions?
                                                                  * Which keywords are driving conversions?

                                                                  * There are many different attribution models where many are based on rules not model 
                                                                  * Rules are generally based on firms' intuition on how each channel plays a role in the purchase funnel of customers 
                                                                  * Last touch attribution: all credit goes to final touchpoint before purchase 
                                                                  * First touch: all credit goes to first touchpoint 
                                                                  * Last non direct touch: ignore direct visits as customers were probably convinced by something else
                                                                  * Uniform: all channels that were interacted with prior to purchase get equal credit 
                                                                  * Exponential: more credit to touchpoints that occurred near the purchase 
                                                                  * Position based: Gives 40% to the first touch, 40% to the last, and splits the remaining 20% among everything in between
                                                                  * Weighted: each touchpoint gets a predetermined weight based on something like historical performance 


                                                                  * Li & Kannan (2014): standard attribution models only look at the last touchpoint, but they ignore the fact that prior channel visits have carryover effects (a visit through channel X today makes you more likely to return through that same channel tomorrow) and spillover effects (visiting through channel X today makes you more likely to visit through channel Y later).
                                                                  * Carryover: within the channel 
                                                                  * Spillover: across channel
                                                                  * Simple rule-based models can't explain why some customers convert while others dont as they treat these customers the same. Li & Kannan's three-level model actually uses individual-level data on customers’ touches and model purchasing and visiting stage 


                                                                  * De Haan, Wiesel & Pauwels (2016) work with aggregated daily data and use a VAR (Vector Autoregression) model, which is a time-series technique. Customer-initiated contacts are more powerful than firm-initiated ones like TV or radio. Content-integrated CICs (like price comparison sites) are the most effective of all.
