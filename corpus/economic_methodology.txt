Ch.1 - Humean
Lecture 1
* Positive science: explains facts that can be proven as right or wrong -> objective 
* Example: Increasing taxes on the rich reduces income inequality (can be tested)
* Normative sceince involves values, opinions and judgements -> subjective  
* Example: The government should increase taxes on the rich (judgement).
* Received views: theories based on observable facts 
* Received view is the concept of science developed by logical positivisim 
* The main goal is demarcation (science vs non-science): scientific knowledge must be logically consistent and verifiable by observation
* What counts as scientific knowledge?
* Analytic propositions: true by definition/logical analysis and dont depend on experience 
-> Examples: all bachelors are unmarried or logical and mathematical truths like circles are round
* Synthetic a posteriori propositions: not true by definition and must be empirically verifiable through observation
-> Examples: my neighbors dog is aggressive or unemployment increased last quarter 
* What does NOT count as scientific knowledge:
* Synthetic a priori propositions: truth is not shown by empirical research and that are not true by definition. Statements you claim are true before you gathered evidence 
-> Example: Newton’s laws and Euclidean geometry are not universally true, but empirically testable and revisable.


* Verifiability Principle (meaningfulness): all propositions that are not true by definition (non analytic statement) are only meaningful iff it can be verified by observation (emperically verified) 
* Consequences are that metaphysical, ethical and religious statement are meaningless for science 
-> example: god created the world in 6 days -> meaningless in science


* logitical positivists are normative scientists because they are making a value of judgement about science itself by saying that only satatements that can be tested or verified are meangingful. IOW, logical positivists are saying science SHOULD only study facts but the type of science they want is positive science


* Empiricism: all evidence for synthetic must come from sense perception
* Statements has to be empirically verifiable 
* Statement has been verified 
* Predicates are meaningful only if we can observe whether something falls under them 
* Example: “is red” 


* Theory structure - the syntactic view:
* A scientific theory is written in formal logic (like math) built from axioms (basic laws) and definitions 
* It is divided into:
* -> Observation statements (sometimes called protocol sentences): they are directly observable facts 
* ->Theoretical statements: things we can’t directly observe like electron, force, intelligence 
* Logical positivists were worried that if we can’t observe these things does it make it meaningless?
* To avoid metaphysics theoretical terms are allowed only if they can be connected to observation


* Operationalization: for a concept to be meaningful it must be entirely in terms of measurement procedure 
* Turns an abstract concept into something that can be measured or observed.
* Correspondence rules (key idea): bridge between theoretical and observation terms 
* How theoretical concepts show up in observable measurements 
* Example: theoretical terms -> temperature & observation term -> thermometer reading 
* Basically: Under the syntactic view, a scientific theory is a formal system of sentences in logical language, containing theoretical and observational terms, where theoretical terms are only meaningful through correspondence rules that link them to observable facts.
* Axioms + theoretical rules -> (via correspondence rules) observable predictions -> emperical testing 


* Theories vs evidence:
* Context of discovery: how a theory was found -> irrelevant 
* Context of justification: how a theory is logically and empirically supported -> crucial 
-> science only concerns justificiation


* The Deductive-Nomological (DN) answers the question: what does it mean to scientifically explain something?
* According to the received view: to explain an event is not to tell a story it is to logically show that the event had to happen given certain laws
* The idea is that if the event can be deduced then it is explained 
* How to remember it: nomos-> law
* So, an explanation = deduction using laws not just facts
* DN: Law + initial conditions = event (explained)
* Example 1: why did the stone fall?
* Law: all objects near the earth experience gravitational acceleration 
* Condition: the stone was released near earth 
* Explanation: the stone fell
* Example 2: why did unemployment increase?
* Law: if aggregate demand decreases, unemployment increases 
* Condition: AD fell this quarter 
* Explanation: unemployment increased this quarter 


* Scientific Laws must be:
* Universal: must apply to all objects 
* Exceptionless: if one exception exists its not a law
* Unrestricted by time/place: true everywhere, always 
* Necessary: not a coincidence 
-> Example of law: all bodies near earth accelerate at 9.8 m/s^2 (in a vacuum)
-> Example accidental: all coins in my pocket are silver 
* BUT they cannot be satisfactorily defined semantically and induction cannot justify them with certainty 
* Induction: the inference of a general law from particular instances


* The problem of induction (Humean)
* Observe many cases -> infer general law
-> example: observed that the sun rose every day so far -> sun will rise tom
* No logical guarantee that the future will resemble the past as no amunt of observations can logically prove a universal law 
* This is a problem because law must be universal and exceptionless but observation can only give finite data 
* so how can we justified universal laws scientifically? We can’t do that with certainty 
* Logical positivist responses: not a solution but tried to manage it 
* Intrumentalism (Moritz Schilick): laws are useful tools and guidelines not literal truths; dont claim theyre true, only effective 
* Confirmation theory (Rudolph Carnap): evidence doesn’t prove laws; it confirms them to a degree -> probabilistic certainty 
* So laws are more of less supported but never absolutely certain
* takeaway : scientific knowledge is fallible, probabilistic and always open to revision


Summary 
* Science is a logical system built from axioms and laws expressed in formal languages connected to the world via observation free from values 
* Scientific if its logically consistent and all empirical terms are operationalized 
* In science only justification matters 
Ch.2 - Samuelson & Friedman
Lecture 2 
* Received view in economics 
* Problem is that economic law cant be tested in labs
* Econometrics was established to provide an alternative to experimental science by unifying economic theory, statistics, and mathematics
* The process of econometrics involves 3 steps 
* Step 1: specify economic model in explicitly functional form (often linear)
* Step 2: decide on the appropriate data definitions and gather relevant data series for every variable included in the model (like operationalization)
* Step 3: create a bridge between theory and data using statistical methods. Bridge consists of various sets of stats, which helps determine the validity of the theoretical model 
* Positivism in econometrics:
* mathematics as analytical foundation (model structure)
* Statistics as observational foundation (model test)
-> operationalisation and data choice guarantee verifiablity
-> stats testing verifies it 
* Explanation and prediction follows D-N model 


* The Keyenes-Tinbergen Debate
* Tinbergen’s methods: trying to make a model of the whole american economy with many variables 
* Keyenes’ critique: tinbergens method only assumes the theory is correct and simply fills in the numbers. It failed as a test because it could not discover new causes or critize existing model if it doesnt provide a complete list of all significant casual factors beforehand.
* Basically, tinbergen doesnt test theory but simply measures  
* Tinbergens measuring is only possible if all relevant causal factors are already known 


* Haavelmo thought that we can tweak parameters until we find stability in the parameters which could mean the model is correct and stumble upon an economic law 
* Goal is finding invariance through repeated measurements under different model specifications, but of and by means of probability not exact values 
* Basically economics lacks controlled experiments and stable citerus paribus conditions so his solution was to use prob theory and accept randomness explicitly
* Searches for invariance no exact laws 


* Measurement-without-Theory (Koopmans):
* Accused researchers of only measuring and only describing what the data looks like without explaining why. 
* He argued that true scientific testing required economic theory to specify structural relations. Without a theoretical framework to specific probability distributions and causal directions, statistical results cant be tested for invaraince (whether they will hold true in the future under different policies)


* Cowles vs NBER 
* NBER (Burnss & Mitchell): theory is based on observation. So, you start from data and observation, look for empirical regularities, minimal theory -> they behaved as if theory emerged from data 
* Cowless commision tradition (Koopmans & Haavelmo): start from economic theory, build structural models then test them with data. Theory tells you which variables matter and how they are connected. 
* Koopmans' critique of NBER: economic analysis must start with theory to decide what to observe, and empirical evidence then refines that theory. Data alone isn't enough
* Problems of econometrics 
* No lab -> no certainties of cause and effect 
* More variables -> more certainty and more complexity 
* Do observations (NBER) or introspective theory (Koopmans) determine relevance?
* Either way: the more statistical controls the better -> complexity is unavoidable


* Friedman’s methodology
* Friedman claimed that if models out predict the benchmarks of naive models, then it is a good model. 
* Models are simplifications and assumptions can be unrealistic 
* Validity of a model should be judge solely by its predictive success not realism of assumptions
* Dont test assumptions test predictions 


* Alfred marshall said that nature is so complex and we cannot solve everything simultaneously so we carefully analyse one element at a time and combine partial results into an approximate overall understanding.
* Friedman uses the same logic but explicitly in economics. A full theory of economic change it so complex to build directly so you should analyse specific sectors and combine them to coherent general theories 
* Friedman claimed that important theories often rely on highly unrealistic assumptions but still yield good predictions
* Unrealistic assumptions for examplewhen economics assumes perfectly rational agents. This assumption is false literally, but useful analytically.
* Types of assumptions (Musgrave) -> defends Friedman against the charge of false assumptions:
* Domain assumptions: define where theory applies 
* Negligibility assumptions: factors exist but have no effect 
* Heuristic assumptions: made if a factor is considered negligible just to simplify the logical development of the theory 


* Summary of friedman:
* Simplify for parts (or domains) as economy is too complex and test by predictions 
* Positivist aspects: still accepts D-N model so we still believes that science explains by laws
* Distances himself from testing assumptions due to induction which breaks logical positivism 
* Relevance of observation is evaluated solely by predictive success within specific domains
* (for him the model is never wrong its just that we applied it to a different domain)
* Instead of increasinging complexity you just get more and more models 
* If you can predict something -> it is scientificic
* Critique of F-twist (Samuelson):
* Prediction does not mean explanation 
* Two false models can predict equally well
* Examples
App A says: “It will rain tomorrow because the clouds are angry.”
App B says: “It will rain tomorrow because a magic frog croaked.”
Both repdicts rain only 1 makes sense 
* Without realism we cannot compare theories meaningfully and learn causal mechanisms 
* Replacing explanations just means better description not deeper truths 
* Leads to descriptivism
* Descriptivism:
* economics becomes pattern fitting and no true laws 
* If all elements must be empirically verified economic laws become impossible 
* No D-N explanations only descriptions of pattern




* summary : 
* Anti-realism in Friedman’s methodology holds that the assumptions of economic models don’t need to be descriptively accurate, as they function merely as analytical tools. 
* What matters is the predictive success of the model, especially out-of-sample. Given the complexity of economic reality and the impossibility of directly testing assumptions, unrealistic simplifications are both unavoidable and desirable.
* Friedman says that powerful scientific theories rely on deliberately unrealistic assumptions in order to abstract from irrelevant details and generate simple, general, and testable predictions, and should be evaluated by their predictive success rather than the literal truth of their assumptions. 
* However, this instrumentalist view is criticised for undermining explanation and causal understanding, potentially reducing economics to mere description rather than law-like science.
Ch.3 - Popper & Duhem-Quine
Lecture 3
* Remember the received view says that science starts with facts and from many observations we induce general laws
* Induction problem states that no amount of observation can prove a universal law (black swan example)


* Popper’s Solution: Falsificationism 
* Idea: science doesn't prove theories true it tries to prove them false 
* Scientific procedure by Popper:
* Propose a bold conjecture (general theory)
* Clearly state what should falsify it
* Test theory aggressively 
* If it survives keeps it as provisional (never as final truth)
-> basically, theories are never verified, they are only not yet falsified 
* Implications:
* Laws are back 
* Scientific knowledge is fallible (can be mistaken)
* Scientists should aim for refutations rather than verifications


* Why falsibility matters (demarcation problem)
* Scientific theories make risk predictions and could be proven wrong 
-> einsteins relativity -> risky, testable predictions -> scientific 
-> had a way to prove it wrong 
* Non-scientific theories explain everything and therefore explain nothing; can never be refuted 
-> freud/adler -> any outcome confirms the theory -> non scientific 
-> can never be proven wrong


* Testing theories Popper-style 
* Logical consistency (min req)
* Falsifiability (demarcation criterion)
* Empirical consequences (predictions)
* Empirical content (potential falsifiers)
* Empirical content: how much a theory say about the world 
* More predictions -> more potential falsifiers -> more phenomena covere
* Empirical content increases -> more precise predictions are or more general class can be tested upon
* If something is more likely to be true it is less likely to be falsified so it has less empirical content 
* Example: IR falls vs IR falls and keeps falling until it reaches its natural level 
* The second is riskier and has higher empirical content so is a better science 
* Logical positivists: more probable = better theory 
* Popper: more improbable but informative = better theory 
* A theory that says almost nothing is very likely true but a theory that says a lot risks being wrong 


* Making certain observation and base theories on it then we make sure that every observation/theory is totally true so growth of knowledge is exponential 
* Popper: Scientific knowledge doesn't grow by accumulating proven truths, but by eliminating errors and producing theories that are less wrong, more precise, and more informative. So proving a theory is false doesnt decrease knowledge as we now know what is false and where the old theory fails. 


* Theory ladeness observation
* With popper theory comes before observations 
* Theories offers potential falsifiers and tells us what to look for 
* Observations are guided by theory 
* Facts are not neutral they are interpreted through theoretical lenses


* Immunizing strategies (how theories avoid falsification)
* Scientists often protect theories instead of abandoning them 
* A posteriori strategies (after bad evidence): redefine concepts, adjust underlying theories, change domain 
* A priori strategy: “all else equal” -> i used vaguely it makes theories unfalsifiable 
* Popper warns: saving a theory at all costs is bad science 
* Duhem-Quine problem
* When a prediction fails, what is falsified? Core theory? measurement? domain?
* If we do not know what is at fault, immunizing strategems may actually improve the theory
* Example: whales were classified as fish but when it was discovered that they breathe air and can drown. Instead of redefining the definition of fish they were reclassified to mammals. so in this case saving the theory improved it


* Logical positivism: the more probable a theory is the more reason to accept it 
* Popper: the more improbable the theory is the more reason to accept it 
* Friedman: the more significant the theory is, the more unrealistic its assumptions


* Social laws and economics:
* Popper is skeptical about strict laws in social science because theyre hard to find as there are no controlled experiments and many factors 
* Reflexivity: once people know the law they change behavior and invalidate it
-> there will be a bank run so people will rush to withdraw money so the prediction causes the event 
-> cant happen in physics: electrons dont change behavior when physicists publish papers 😂
* Social and historical inescapability (that history follows fixed laws independent of human choices) (such as in Hegel’s and Marx’s historicism) does not exist because you cannot predict the future of history asit depends on future knowledge, and future knowledge cannot be known in advance. Prediction a future discovery today means its not really a discovery since you already know it 


* Political implications: the open society:
* Popper links epistemology to politics
* An open society:
- Allows criticism 
- Encourage public debate
- improves institutions gradually
Rejects authoritarian historical laws 
* Science and democracy both progress through criticism not certainty 


* Summary:
* Popper argues that scientific knowledge grows not by verifying theories but by proposing bold, falsifiable conjectures and eliminating errors through critical testing 
Ch.4 - Kuhn & Lakatos
Lecture 4:
* The received view says that science is objective facts + logic and that they are neutral 
-> not really the case
* This view cannot explain why smart scientists disagree so deeply or why they miss big events
* Kuhn wonders if facts are objective, why did aristotle’s wrong ideas survive for centuries?
* Kuhn’s answer is that science is a social activity not just logic + data 
* Theoretical frameworks held by observer influences observations
* What scientists see depends on what they already believe in 
* So, scientists work inside a community


* Normal science takes place within a scientific community that shares 
* Research domain (what is worth studying)
* Methods (how to study it)
* Criteria for good science
* This shared framework is what kuhn calls a paradigm 
* Once a paradigm exists scientists don't question it they only start solving because everyone agrees on these things. They dont discover new things; the only work within the paradigm
* Example: no one asks why should we even use equilibrium this question lies outside normal science 
* Kuhn breaks down the paradigm into a disciplinary matrix (everuthong a scientific community shares that lets them do normal science together) 
* Paradigm: standard examples everybody learns; template for problem solving
-> the Marshallian supply–demand graph
* Metaphysics: background worldview, not tests directly 
-> agents respond to incentives 
* Symbolic generalizations: equations and symbols everyone accepts without debate 
-> equilibrium conditions 
* Values: shared standards for evaluating theories
* -> formal modeling instead of verbal reasoning 
* Education & policing: why paradigms persist:
* Students learn through standard examples and imitating successful solutions not by questioning fundamentals  
* Even after education, journals, conferences and rankings reward conformity 
* This created intellectual stability but also blind spots aka anomalies 


* So, if paradigms are so stable, how does change ever happen? -> Anomalies 
* Anomalies is a fact the paradigm can’t explain 
* Scientists dont see anomalies because of “Gesalt perception”
* A gesalt image can be a duck or a rabbit but you dont see both at once 
* At first, anomalies are ignored, explained away or treated as measurement errors because facts are interpreted through the paradigm so perception itself is structured 
* This leads to radical conclusion
* So, observation is theory-laden
* There are no neutral facts so they are not independent of theory as observation already presupposes concepts 
* You dont see facts then build theory


* If observation depends on theory and paradigms shape what counts as a fact then there is no neutral language to compare paradigms -> incommensurability 
* A new paradigm leads scientists to view different puzzles (specifically anomalies) in a differen tlight 
* So science doesn't grow smoothly and there are breaks 
* Paradigm change isnt purely rational which leads to scientific revolutions 
* Scientific revolutions:
* When anomalies accumulate -> confidence in paradigm collapses -> a new paradigm emerges -> scientists “swtich Gesalt”
* Problem is that old and new paradigms are incompatible and science starts to look irrational because there is no neutral way to decide between paradigms   
* Lakatos: 
* Kuhn’s idea of a paradigm shift is too extreme, it’s like a “religious conversion.” Scientists suddenly abandon one worldview for another, seemingly irrationally. This makes science look irrational, because there’s no clear rational standard for choosing the new paradigm.
* Poppers falsification is naive; In reality, theories are never rejected immediately just because some predictions fail. Scientists often adjust auxiliary hypotheses instead of abandoning the theory.
* Kuhm + popper = sophisticated falsification
* Lakatos says that you can compare programmes rationallt:
* He agrees with kuhn that facts dont falsify theories and science is not instant falsifications 
* But disagrees that paradigm change is irrational 
* His solution is research programs 
* Research programs = structured paradigms:
* Part 1 - Hard core: fundamental assumptions, never questioned
* Part 2 - Protective belt: adjustable assumptions; can be adjusted when problems arise 
* This explains why theories survive anomalies and scientists dont abandon frameworks immediately 
* Duhem-Quine problem: when data contradict a theory, what failed?
* Lakatos says: immunising strategies are theory improvements in a progressive research programme
* Progressive programs: Predicts new facts thats are later confirmed emperically
* Degenerating programs: Only explains problems after the fact 
* rationality:
* choose most progressive research program
* Theoretical pprogressiveness: predicts/explains new facts 
* Empirically progressives: new facts are actually shown empirically  
* problemL how long before theoretical progressiveness is empirically observed 


* Summary:
* Kuhn explains scientific stability through education and institutional policing, and scientific change through anomalies that trigger paradigm shifts. 
* Kuhn: Science works in “paradigms”, long periods of normal science, punctuated by sudden paradigm shifts 
* Kuhm: something is true if it conforms to social conventions regarding truth
* Because observation is theory-laden, paradigms are incommensurable and progress is discontinuous. 
* Lakatos accepts this critique of naive falsification but restores rationality by comparing research programmes based on their theoretical and empirical progressiveness.




Ch.5 - SSK & ESK
Lecture 5
* Sociology of scientific knowledge (SSK) asks: why do scientists believe certain theories and reject others 
* According do SSK, knowledge is not justified by abstract rational rules but on theories and concepts that are established and accepted. In other words, it is these interests that determine what scientists come to regard as knowledge.
* SSK claims that knowledge production serves social wants; uses sociological techniques to understand the practice of science
* truth is socially constructed
* Its not made up but what counts as truth depends on shared standards and community agreement 
* Kuhn fits here: scientists are trained to see the world in a certain way (Gesalt) and operate within communities not individuals 
* Leads to the naturalistic turn: Instead of judging science by abstract philosophical rules, we study science using empirical methods, just like we study any other human activity.
* Traditional philosophy focuses on the rules of proper scientific method and such things as logic, truth, evidence, etc that influence the acceptance or rejection of theories 
-> whats true or false are theories of science 
* SSK: focuses on scientists’ behaior in terms of motives and interests as members of scientific communities to understand why theories are accepted/rejected 
-> what’s true or false are theories of scientists’ behavior 


* The edinburgh strong programme 
* Starting point: study science naturalistically 
* Scientists are to be investigated just as we would natural phenomena -> try to be understood objectively
* Instead of asking “Is this belief true?” it asks: “Why did scientists come to believe this?”
* Four principles to prevent passing of judgement:
1. Causality: explains why scientists hold the beliefs they do
2. Impartiality: treat true and false beliefs equally
3. Symmetry: same types of causes explain success and failure
4. Relexivity: apply the SSK to SSK itself; No one gets a free epistemic pass, not even us.
-> every time u find a reason for T/F you gained an understanding of the beliefs but still stuck in believing that some beliefs are T/F and those beliefs are caused by your own scienitifc community so needs to be researched the same way as other communities 
* Another approach - Lab studies approach (microsociological SSK):
* SSK doesn’t just theorise, it focuses on daily practices in labs and workplaces -> Its an immersive approach 
* Basically goes undercover in these scientific communities 
* Actor Network theory (Callon):
* Science produced by networks of humans (Scientists) and non-humans (machines, etc.)
* Knowledge stabilises through alliances 


* Philosophical problems raised by SSK:
* Relativism: truth depends on historical and social context 
-> you try to make rules to get a truthful description of the community but then say that these rules are social constructs -> reflexivity?
-> hard to be objective 
* Realism (world exists independently of beliefs): SSK leans towards relativism so can we say that SSK itself is objectively true?
* Value neutrality: SSK claims to be descriptive and value neutral but topic choice and concept used are value-laden 


* ES(K): economic techniques to understand the practice of science -> individual interests must be aligned with scientific values 
* Both ESK & SSK explain science socially but while SSK uses sociology ESK uses economics 
* Scientists are modeled as utility maximizers
* 2 versions: 
* Old economics of science (ES): Scientists follow certain characteristic norms. The way its organized can make it easier or more difficult to do so but doesnt change as a reaction to incentives 
* New economics of scientific knowledge (ESK): scientific output depends on incentives that scientists face -> what they do is affected by how science is organized


* Old economics of science: Robert Merton
* 4 norms for good science -> cudos 
1. Communalism: common ownership of scientific discoveries -> scientists give up intellectual property (will never keep it to themselves) in exchange for recognition and esteem 
2. Universalism: good scientists will demonstrate truth of statements and theories in a way that are universally acceptable  -> should not depend on who is making it 
3. Disinterestedness: scientists shouldn't act for personal financial gains and are rewarded for acting in ways that outwardly appear to be selfless. Scientists should act for the advancement of knowledge
4. Organized skepticism: scientific claims should be tested and be exposed to rigorous, structured community scrutiny 


* This benefit society but not scientists themselves. They work to create universally acceptable truth, disseminating to wide audience, are subjected to lots of criticism while trying to be disinterested
-> creates pos externalities but are underproduced in competitive markets 
* So it is a pure public good -> everyone benefits except scientists themselves -> must be subsidized or be given patents for applied research which can be profitable 


* New economics of scientific knowledge: david and dasgupta
* Treats scientists as economics agents that maximize utility specifically reputation
* Open science makes it rational for self interested scientists to behave in ways that still produce good knowledge 
* Open science is incentive compatible with a reward system based on self interest
* This includes quickly publishing results, public access and priority rule where credit goes to first discoverer 
* Scientists would publish their work quickly because thats the only way to get recognition and maximize reputation (communalism), must appeal to a wide group (universalism), must seem disinterested to not take a hit on your reputation (disinterestedness), and must make sure you dont have mistakes so you ask people to critize before publishing (organised scrutiny)
* So in the end if they want to maximize reputation they will have to go through the 4 norms of Merton
=> science becomes break-through oriented 
* The standards of research push younger researchers to conform to survive as alternative approaches struggle to enter.
* So by rewarding conformity, dominant approaches persist even when better alternatives exist.
* Causes lock-in problems: Technologies and ideas persist even if inefficient 
* Example: QWERTY keyboard 
* Philip kitcher’s social epistemology:
* Science is a social study so scientific standards are also social 
* Goal: maximize ratio of reliable beliefs to total beliefs in population
* How?: arrange social institutions in most epistemically (relating to knowledge or degree of validation) efficient way possible 
* Principle: knowledge is promoted by cognitive diversity through competition 
* Intuition: an industrial organization of cognitive labor -> divide intellectual labor
* Basically, different people tackle different problems, using diverse methods, competing and cooperating, so that collectively we produce the most reliable knowledge.


* Summary:
* SSK: emphasizes communities and social interests; uses sociological techniques 
* ESK: emphasizes incentives and self interest; uses economic techniques 
* Old economics of science (ES): basic research is a public good with positive externalities -> subsidize 
=> normative approach to science 
* New economics of science (ESK): scientists are utility maximizers


Ch. 6 - Postmodernism & rhetoric
Lecture 6
* Postmodernism is a critique of humanism and traditional views of science dating from the enlightenment 
* Postmodernism questions the basic assumptions of modern science 
* It is a rejection of modernism and rejects ideas like:
* Theres a single scientific method: there is one correct method for producing scientific knowledge 
-> post modernism says that there is no single method and that methodology is plural not universal
* Knowledge has foundations 
-> PM there are no ultimate unquestionable foundations as observations are theory-laden and what counts as evidence changes over time
* Meaning can ultimately be determined with certainty: once defined clearly, everyone should agree
-> PM: meaning is context-dependent and plural 
* History possess a pattern such as progress
-> PM: history is non-linear and messy 
* The self is a coherent unity 
-> PM: the “self” is socially constructed so individuals dont have stable identities 
* Things and beings have essential natures 
-> PM: you can say that you made observations that tells you the truth about something, however you made a lot of assumptions that you dont know are true 
* Master narratives are possible
-> PM: no single story explains everything
* Causal determination has a privileged role in explanation
->PM: not everything has a clear stable cause 
* Things can be represented in language: if we choose the right words or equations, we represent the world as it truly is 
-> PM: language shapes reality instead of mirroring it  
* Implication of postmodernism:
* Demarcation is a non-starter. Draws a clear line between science vs non science 
* PM says this is impossible as there are many ways of describing and interpreting reality, none uniquely superior 
* PM also critiques economic theory itself 
-> equilibrium: there may be many equilibria, unstable equilibria or none at all 
-> causality: economic change may be random and arbitrary -> coincidental 
-> rationality: the single rational self may not exist
* Leads to rhetoric approach 
* Positivism (according to McCloskey)
* Positivism is a misleading and overly narrow view of science 
* McCloskey argues that positivism treats economics as a science that should rely only on logic, numbers, and observable evidence, while ignoring persuasion, judgment, rhetoric, and real scientific practice.
* She characterized positivism as (she disagreed with this):
1. Science aims at predictions and control: goal of economics is to predict not understand or interpret social reality 
2. Only observable implications matter for truth: A theory is judged only by its observable predictions, not by realism of assumptions, interpretation, or narrative plausibility
3. Knowledge must be based on objective observation: scientific knowledge must come from observable, reproducible objective experiments. Subjectivity is excluded 
4. Falsification is decisive: A theory is scientific only if it can be falsified, and only when a prediction fails is the theory rejected.
5. Objectivity is essential: subjective elements are considered unscientific 
6. Quantification is superior (Kelvin’s Dictum): Knowledge that cannot be expressed numerically is viewed as inferior or unscientific.
7. Discovery vs justification: nonscientific elements may help discover ideas but can never justify thejm 
8. Sharp demarcation of science: methodology’s role is to separate science vs non science 
9. Explanation means subsumption under laws: To explain something scientifically is to show it follows from a general law.
10. Science is value-free: Economists, as scientists, have nothing to say about moral or aesthetic values.
* McCloskey says that economics is not a science or social science but humanities
* Uses a classical rhetoric approach 
* Traditional concern: this approach was usually used as methods of argument and communication 
* Modern focus: used as discourse analysis (how people speak)
* Connections to SSK: emphasis on the practices of scientists and their tools 
* Classical rhetoric:
* Logos: logical appeals (orignally)
* Pathos: emotional appeals
* Ethos: apeals to speaker’s sincerity and authority 
* The rhetoric approach: persuasive
* Attention to how economists actually seek to persuade one another
* Economics doesnt develop through simple rules but through communication between economists
-> mathematics gives authority (adds to ethos not logos)
-> writing ideas mathematically adds to how impressive argument looks so people take it more seriously 
* Analogy persuades by interpreting economic statements in terms of another more intuitive framework 
-> interactive competition seens as game theory 
* Metaphor persuades by importing non-economic concepts into economics (
Vice versa)

The received view
According to this view, facts exist independently of theories, scientists propose theories which are tested against facts 
In economics, this translated into: models generate predictions data tests those predictions. If predictions fail → model is rejected

Popper: falsification
Popper argued: you can never prove a theory true, you can only try to falsify it
Science progresses by eliminating false theories
But in economics: models are rarely abandoned after one failed prediction, assumptions are tweaked instead. “Auxiliary assumptions” protect the core model

Kuhn: paradigms
Kuhn claimed that facts are not neutral and are interpreted within paradigms. What counts as a “problem” or “solution” depends on the dominant framework and what is widely accepted

   * Keynesians vs New Classicals interpret the same data differently
   * No single experiment settles the debate


Lakatos: research programmes
Lakatos tried to save rationality by saying that science consists of research programmes
   * A “hard core” protected by auxiliary hypotheses
   * Progress judged by whether a programme generates new insights
This already moves us away from naïve “facts decide everything”.

         * Realism of assumptions is irrelevant
Two Criteria for Theory Evaluation
         1. Precision: More precise predictions are better
         2. Simplicity: Theories using fewer independent variables are better
Dealing with Complexity
         * Limits applicability to specific domains where complicating factors are negligible
         * Prefers many simple models for different domains over one complex universal model
Instrumentalism Critique
         * Theories are merely instruments for prediction
         * Does not distinguish between correlation and causation
         * Critics say this makes it anti-realist
Musgrave's Defense
         * Friedman focuses on domain assumptions and negligibility assumptions
         * If a theory predicts well, it likely captures the most important realistic factors
         * Not necessarily anti-realist, just pragmatic about domain-specific truth
4. SAMUELSON'S DESCRIPTIVISM
Implication vs. Equivalence
         * Friedman: Premises that correctly predict (imply) consequences are scientific
         * Samuelson: Requires premises to be based on and equivalent to all relevant observations
Why Scientific Laws Don't Exist (for Samuelson)
         * General descriptions are based on limited observations
         * Can never be certain laws hold across all time and space
         * Can describe regularities but not establish universal laws
________________


5. POPPER'S FALSIFICATIONISM
Demarcation Criterion
         * Only logically consistent statements that could be shown false by observation, but have not been, are scientific
         * Scientific theories must be falsifiable
Circumventing the Problem of Induction
         * Accepts general disprovable statements that haven't yet been shown false
         * Science progresses by attempting to falsify theories, not verify them
Empirical Content
         * Definition: The number of potential observations that could disprove a theory
         * How to increase it: Make statements more general AND more precise
         * Higher empirical content = more scientific
         * Large domain and precise statement 
Example Ranking:
         1. "If supply rises while demand stays constant, price falls by the same percentage" (Most empirical content - general and precise)
         2. "If supply rises while demand stays constant, price falls" (Medium - general but less precise)
         3. "If potato supply rises, potato prices fall" (Least - specific, not general)
Immunizing Strategies (A Posteriori)
What they are:
         * Re-operationalizing terms after falsification
         * Adding ceteris paribus clauses after the fact
         * Adjusting the domain of applicability to exclude counterexamples
Popper's view: Forbidden - they allow scientists to avoid admitting theories are false
Duhem-Quine Thesis
         * When a theory is falsified, we can't be sure if the theory itself or auxiliary assumptions are at fault
         * Problem for Popper: Makes it unclear what to reject when falsification occurs
Historicism Critique
         * Historicism: Belief in discovering laws of historical development
         * Popper's objection: Social laws are historically and culturally specific, subject to change
         * Positing immutable social laws threatens democratic, open society
Piecemeal Social Engineering
         * Related to fallibilism: all knowledge is uncertain
         * Both scientists and policymakers must be ready to adjust when outcomes differ from expectations
         * Incremental changes preferred over grand social transformations
________________


6. KUHN'S PARADIGMS
Incommensurability
         * Different paradigms have different:
         * Research questions (puzzles)
         * World views
         * Standards for evaluation
         * Scientists in different paradigms cannot agree on important questions or interpretations
         * Makes long-term assessment of knowledge growth impossible
Disciplinary Matrix Components
         1. Symbolic Generalizations: Mathematical formulations (e.g., Max U(x))
         2. Metaphysics: Fundamental beliefs about the world (e.g., "human behavior is goal-oriented")
         3. Values: Standards for good theories (e.g., "theories must be mathematically modeled")
         4. Paradigm: Exemplary problem solutions (e.g., prisoner's dilemma)
Normal Science as Puzzle Solving
         * Scientists assume:
         1. A solution exists
         2. Standard tools of the discipline will find it
         * Like puzzle solving: if you can't solve it, it's your failure, not the puzzle's fault
         * Scientists don't question fundamental assumptions during normal science
Gestalt and Gestalt-Switch
         * Gestalt: The way one sees a multi-interpretable picture
         * Disciplinary training determines a common Gestalt
         * Gestalt-switch: When scientists start seeing things differently - can trigger scientific revolution
Scientific Revolutions
         * Occur when anomalies accumulate and paradigm fails
         * New paradigm emerges with different world view
         * Previous observations may not even be perceivable in new paradigm
________________


7. LAKATOS'S RESEARCH PROGRAMS
Synthesis of Popper and Kuhn
         * From Kuhn: Hard core (like paradigm) - fundamental unfalsifiable assumptions
         * From Popper: Protective belt theories must be falsifiable
Structure of Research Programs
Hard Core
         * Fundamental, unfalsifiable assumptions
         * Example (Neoclassical): "Economy consists of optimizing agents"
Protective Belt
         * Adjustable theories that protect the hard core
         * Can be modified or replaced
Positive Heuristic
         * Guidelines for theory development
         * Example: "Adjust theories to keep hard core intact"
Negative Heuristic
         * Prohibitions
         * Example: "Don't propose theories where equilibrium is meaningless"
Progressive vs. Degenerative Programs
Progressive:
         * Theories in protective belt predict novel facts
         * Attracts more scientists
         * Advances knowledge
Degenerative:
         * Only explains known facts through ad hoc adjustments
         * Loses scientists
         * Stagnates
Solving the Duhem-Quine Problem
         * Unlike Popper, allows adjustment of auxiliary theories
         * Doesn't require rejecting entire framework when falsification occurs
         * Can repair protective belt while maintaining hard core

8. SOCIOLOGY OF SCIENTIFIC KNOWLEDGE (SSK)
The Naturalistic Turn
         * Shift from normative (what science should be) to descriptive (what science actually is)
         * Studies science as a social phenomenon
Edinburgh School Strong Programme
Four Principles:
         1. Causality: Look for reasons behind convictions
         2. Impartiality: Don't pass judgment on beliefs
         3. Symmetry: Same causes can explain true and false beliefs
         4. Reflexivity: Apply same standards to SSK itself
Social Constructivism Problem
         * Claims scientists create truths rather than discover them
         * Truth is relative to the group
         * Self-defeating: If true, SSK's own claims are just social constructions, not objective truths
Laboratory Studies
         * Observe scientists in their actual work environments
         * Studies how scientific facts are constructed through social processes
Actor Network Theory (Callon)
         * Knowledge production depends on personal relations, hierarchies, and organizational ties
         * Charts these networks to understand why certain research occurs

9. ECONOMICS OF SCIENCE
Merton's Four Norms
         1. Universalism: Truth claims based on universal criteria, not personal characteristics
         2. Communalism: Research shared widely and quickly, knowledge is public good
         3. Disinterestedness: Avoid conflicts of interest, pursue knowledge not profit
         4. Organized Skepticism: Welcome well-argued criticism
Merton's Policy Implications
         * Fundamental research is a public good (non-rivalrous, non-excludable)
         * Benefits society more than private actors
         * Should be subsidized by government
         * Open science system is incentive-compatible with fundamental research
Economics of Scientific Knowledge (ESK)
David & Dasgupta's Model:
         * Scientists maximize reputation
         * Do so by publishing breakthrough theories quickly and reliably
         * Open science and priority tournaments facilitate this
         * Emphasizes competition and scientific prizes (like Nobel)
Kitcher's Approach:
         * Science should maximize ratio of reliable beliefs to total beliefs
         * Requires epistemically efficient division of cognitive labor
         * Like Adam Smith's division of labor but for knowledge production
         * Justified beliefs depend on "social standards of belief"

10. RHETORIC AND POSTMODERNISM
Classical Rhetoric (Ethos, Pathos, Logos)
Ethos: Appeal to authority/credibility
         * Example: "As a senior economist at the ECB, I can assure you..."
Pathos: Appeal to emotion
         * Example: "A mask mandate is the first step toward totalitarian oppression"
Logos: Appeal to logic/reason
         * Example: "If prices are inversely related to quantity supplied, then increases in quantity supplied decrease price"
McCloskey's Position
What she advocates:
         * Mainly descriptive: studies how economists actually convince each other
         * Rhetoric matters - all arguments use persuasive techniques
         * Pluralism in methods and language use
Models as Storytelling:
         * Models formalize informally told stories
         * The stories do the convincing, not the mathematics
         * Focus on art of convincing rather than truth criteria
Modernism/Structuralism Assumptions (McCloskey identifies these to reject them)
         1. Single scientific method exists
         2. Knowledge has foundations
         3. Meaning can be determined with certainty
         4. History shows progress
         5. Self is coherent unity
         6. Things have essential natures
         7. Master narratives are possible
         8. Causation privileged in explanation
         9. Things can be represented in language
Postmodernist Rejections
McCloskey agrees with postmodernists:
         * No single method
         * Knowledge lacks foundations
         * Meaning depends on context (pluralism)
McCloskey differs from postmodernists:
         * Still believes more convincing theories are better
         * Emphasizes civil conversation
         * Not complete relativism - quality distinctions matter
Reductionism
         * Quest to find underlying entities and processes explaining all phenomena
         * Assumes essential natures exist and master narratives possible
         * Modernist/structuralist approach
Pluralism Types
Epistemological Pluralism (descriptive):
         * Multiple forms of knowledge exist alongside each other
         * Observation of how things are
Methodological Pluralism (prescriptive):
         * Multiple forms of knowledge ought to exist
         * Advocacy for diversity of approaches

11. VALUES IN ECONOMICS
Three Types of Methodological Value Judgments (Inevitable)
         1. Choice of Subject Matter: What the discipline studies (domain)
         2. Methods of Investigation: How to study phenomena
         3. Standards of Validity: How to evaluate results
Four Ways Values Enter Economics
         1. Inevitability of methodological value judgments: Must choose domain, methods, standards
         2. Value-ladenness of concepts (Myrdal): Terms like "rationality" carry normative weight
         3. Supporting ethical views: Economic theories imply certain behaviors are better
         4. Accommodating people's values: Economic explanations must account for ethical motivations
Hume's Guillotine (Is-Ought Distinction)
         * Cannot derive "ought" statements from "is" statements
         * Descriptive claims don't logically imply prescriptive ones
         * But Myrdal argues strict separation impossible in economics
Weak vs. Strong Value-Ladenness
Weakly Value-Laden:
         * Concept has normative connotations in ordinary language
         * Example: "rationality" generally viewed positively
Strongly Value-Laden:
         * Theory implies moral value judgments
         * Example: Rational choice theory suggests preference satisfaction is ethically superior
Rational Choice Theory Example
         * Starts with weakly value-laden "rationality"
         * Argues rational behavior increases social welfare
         * Concludes people MUST behave rationally
         * Transforms weak into strong value-ladenness
Pareto Principle Critique
         * Often presented as normatively neutral
         * Actually contains strong political judgment
         * Implies: redistribution must be rejected if it causes any inefficiency
         * Privileges efficiency over equity

12. COMPLEX SYSTEMS AND BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS
Behavioral Economics and Postmodernism
         * Finds preferences are time-inconsistent
         * Postmodernist connection: Challenges the self as coherent unity
         * If preferences change, the "self" is not stable and predictable
Complex Adaptive Systems Theory
         * Small changes in initial conditions can have huge, unpredictable effects
         * Postmodernist connection: Challenges privileged role of causal determination
         * Causality functions in seemingly arbitrary ways
         * Equilibrium becomes virtually meaningless

         * Merton vs. David & Dasgupta: Both support public funding, but emphasize different mechanisms (norms vs. reputation incentives)
         * Logical Positivists vs. Popper: Both want empirical foundation, but positivists verify, Popper falsifies
